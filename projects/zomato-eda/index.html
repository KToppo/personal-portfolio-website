Zomato Data Analysis

Understanding how a food-tech platform operates at scale — through data, not assumptions.

This project was created to understand how a large food-tech platform like Zomato operates across cities, cuisines, and customer preferences — using data rather than intuition.

Instead of treating the dataset as a collection of restaurants, I approached it as a business ecosystem involving customers, restaurants, locations, pricing, and ratings.

The objective was simple but important: extract insights that explain platform behavior, not just summarize data.

Exploratory analysis of restaurant distribution, pricing, ratings, and platform features across cities.

How I Approached the Analysis

I split the project into two clear phases: data cleaning and data exploration.

The raw dataset contained inconsistencies, missing values, and mixed formats — a realistic reflection of real-world business data. Cleaning was treated as a first-class task, not a formality, ensuring that downstream insights were reliable.

Once the data was structured, I focused on exploratory analysis to answer business-relevant questions such as:

  • How does restaurant availability vary across cities?
  • Are higher ratings associated with higher pricing?
  • Which cuisines dominate different regions?
  • How do online delivery and table booking affect customer perception?

Rather than plotting everything, I filtered insights based on decision relevance — what would matter to the platform, restaurant partners, or customers.

Key Insights and Observations

The analysis highlighted several important patterns:

  • Strong regional differences in cuisine dominance and pricing behavior
  • Cities with dense restaurant listings show higher competition and rating spread
  • Premium pricing does not always correlate with higher customer ratings
  • Online delivery and table booking features influence visibility and engagement

These insights help explain why platform strategies differ city by city, and why blanket assumptions rarely work in marketplace businesses.

What This Project Actually Demonstrates

  • End-to-end EDA on a real-world marketplace dataset
  • The ability to clean and structure messy business data
  • Translating raw metrics into platform-level insights
  • Thinking in terms of stakeholders — customers, restaurants, and the platform

It also reinforced a key analytical habit: good analysis prioritizes relevance over volume.

Why This Project Matters in My Portfolio

This project strengthened my ability to:

  • Analyze consumer-facing platform data
  • Ask business-oriented questions
  • Communicate insights clearly without overclaiming

Alongside my financial and health EDA projects, it demonstrates versatility — applying the same analytical discipline across very different domains.

This analysis doesn’t try to “optimize Zomato”. It focuses on understanding how such platforms behave, how customer preferences vary, and why data-driven decisions matter in competitive marketplaces. That understanding is the real value.
